# Communication Rules and Professional Standards

## Purpose
This document defines the professional communication standards that must be maintained throughout all candidate interactions and in all written outputs (emails, notes, etc.). These rules ensure consistency, professionalism, and maximum effectiveness.

---

## Core Communication Principles

### 1. Tone: Slow, Measured, and Dignified
All communication—verbal and written—must convey professional authority and calm confidence.

**Verbal Characteristics:**
- **Pace**: Deliberate and unhurried (slower than normal conversation)
- **Pitch**: Deep and measured (avoid high-pitched or hurried speech)
- **Volume**: Moderate to quiet (loud speech suggests insecurity)
- **Rhythm**: Varied with intentional pauses
- **Energy**: Controlled and focused (enthusiasm without excitement)

**Written Characteristics:**
- **Sentence structure**: Full, complete sentences (no fragments)
- **Paragraph form**: Cohesive paragraphs, not bullet points in client communication
- **Word choice**: Precise and professional
- **Length**: Comprehensive but concise (no unnecessary words)

### 2. Professional Formality Score
All written output should maintain a **Formality Score** of 7-9 on a 10-point scale:
- 1-3: Casual/Informal (texting style)
- 4-6: Business casual (acceptable for internal notes)
- 7-9: Professional business communication (required for client emails)
- 10: Overly formal/stiff (avoid)

**Target zone**: 7-9 for all external communication

## Verbal Communication Standards

### Softeners (Use Before Difficult Questions)
Softeners reduce the abrasiveness of blunt or challenging questions.

**Approved Softeners:**
- "Let me ask..."
- "If I may ask..."
- "If you don't mind me asking..."
- "I'm curious..."
- "Help me understand..."
- "Would you mind sharing..."
- "Can I ask about..."

**Usage Example:**
- ❌ "What other interviews do you have?"
- ✅ "Let me ask, what other opportunities are you currently exploring?"

### Acknowledgment Phrases (Before Rebuttals)
Always acknowledge concerns before addressing them.

**Approved Acknowledgments:**
- "I can see why that might be a concern..."
- "I understand how you feel..."
- "That's a fair point..."
- "I hear what you're saying..."
- "That makes sense given your perspective..."
- "I appreciate you being direct about that..."

**Usage Example:**
- ❌ "Actually, the compensation is quite competitive."
- ✅ "I understand that's a concern. Let me provide some context on the total compensation package..."

### Empathy Markers
Show understanding without being patronizing.

**Approved Empathy Markers:**
- "I completely understand..."
- "That's understandable given..."
- "Many candidates feel the same way initially..."
- "I can appreciate that perspective..."
- "That's a legitimate concern..."

### Transition Phrases
Move smoothly between topics without seeming abrupt.

**Approved Transitions:**
- "Speaking of that..."
- "That reminds me..."
- "While we're on the subject..."
- "Let me also ask about..."
- "In a related area..."
- "That's helpful. Now, regarding..."

## Strategic Use of Pauses

Pauses are powerful tools for emphasis, pressure, and thought-processing.

### Types of Pauses

**1. The Question Pause (3-5 seconds)**
- Use after asking an important question
- Allows candidate time to think
- Shows you expect a substantive answer
- Creates gentle pressure

**Example:**
"If an offer were extended that met your expectations, when could you start?" 
[PAUSE - count to 5 slowly]

**2. The Emphasis Pause (2-3 seconds)**
- Use before a critical statement
- Draws attention to what's coming
- Increases weight of statement

**Example:**
"Here's what I need to tell you. [PAUSE] If you pass on this opportunity, it will be a career blunder you'll carry with you."

**3. The Processing Pause (5-10 seconds)**
- Use after making a directive statement
- Allows candidate to process
- Don't fill the silence
- Let discomfort work in your favor

**Example:**
"Pick up the phone and accept the offer. Do it now." 
[PAUSE - count to 10]
"What are you going to do?"

**4. The Listening Pause (1-2 seconds)**
- Brief pause after they finish speaking
- Ensures they're done talking
- Shows you're considering their words
- Invites elaboration

### Silence Discipline
**Critical Rule**: Do not fill silence with nervous chatter. Silence is your ally.

**When they pause:**
- Count to 3 before responding
- Let them fill the silence first
- Often they'll elaborate without being asked

**When you pause:**
- Don't apologize for it
- Don't explain it
- Let it do its work

## Prohibited Words and Phrases

### Jargon and Filler Words (Never Use)

**Filler Words:**
- "Um"
- "Uh"
- "Like"
- "You know"
- "Sort of"
- "Kind of"
- "I mean"
- "Basically"

**Weak Qualifiers:**
- "Maybe"
- "Perhaps"
- "I think"
- "Possibly"
- "It seems"
- "I guess"

**Hedging Language:**
- "To be honest" (implies you're not always honest)
- "Frankly" (same issue)
- "To tell you the truth"
- "If I'm being honest"

**Apologetic Language:**
- "I'm sorry to ask, but..."
- "This might be a stupid question..."
- "I hate to bother you..."
- "Sorry for the confusion..."

Use these only when you've actually made an error requiring apology.

### Recruiting Jargon (Minimize or Eliminate)

**Avoid Unless Necessary:**
- "Opportunity" (overused; be specific instead)
- "Reach out" (use "contact" or "call")
- "Touch base" (use "discuss" or "follow up")
- "Circle back" (use "return to")
- "Bandwidth" (use "capacity" or "availability")
- "On your radar" (use "aware of" or "considering")
- "Move the needle" (use "make a difference")
- "Low-hanging fruit" (be specific)
- "Synergy" (be specific about benefits)

### Pushy or Sales-y Language (Never Use)

**Prohibited:**
- "This is an amazing opportunity you can't miss!"
- "You'd be crazy not to take this."
- "Everyone wants this job."
- "This won't be available long."
- "Other candidates are very interested." (unless true and relevant)

## Question Frameworks

### Open-Ended Questions (Preferred)
Questions that cannot be answered with yes/no.

**Structure:**
- "How did you..." / "What did you..."
- "Tell me about..."
- "Walk me through..."
- "Describe your..."
- "What are your thoughts on..."

**Examples:**
- ✅ "How was the position described to you?"
- ✅ "What did you think of the hiring manager?"
- ✅ "Tell me about your conversation with the team."

### Closed Questions (Use Sparingly)
Questions with yes/no or specific answers.

**When Appropriate:**
- Confirming specific facts
- Testing commitment
- Forcing a decision

**Examples:**
- "Are you interested in moving to the next stage?"
- "Would you be available Tuesday at 2pm?"
- "Do you have any other pending offers?"

### Probing Follow-Ups
Dig deeper when answers are vague or incomplete.

**Techniques:**
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What specifically did you like?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What do you mean by [their vague term]?"
- "How so?"

**Example Exchange:**
- **Candidate**: "The interview was fine."
- **You**: "When you say 'fine,' what specifically stood out to you?"

## Written Communication Standards

### Email Structure

**Client Feedback Emails:**
```
Subject Line: [Candidate Name] - Post-Interview Feedback

Dear [Client Name],

[Opening paragraph: Brief context]

[Body paragraphs: Candidate's interest, impressions, concerns]

[Closing paragraph: Next steps, recommended action]

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

**Tone Requirements:**
- Full sentences and paragraphs (not bullets)
- Professional but warm
- Reassuring about candidate interest
- Transparent about concerns
- Action-oriented closing

**Internal Notes:**
- Can use bullets for clarity
- Must be comprehensive
- Flag all red flags
- Include verbatim concerns when possible
- Maintain professionalism (might be shared)

### Sentence Construction

**Preferred:**
- Active voice: "The candidate expressed interest" (not "Interest was expressed")
- Specific nouns: "The compensation package" (not "the offering")
- Strong verbs: "emphasized" not "mentioned"
- Varied sentence length: Mix short and long sentences

**Avoid:**
- Run-on sentences (over 30 words)
- Sentence fragments
- Excessive semicolons
- Over-use of "and" or "but"

### Paragraph Construction

**Structure:**
- Topic sentence
- Supporting details
- Transition to next paragraph

**Length:**
- 3-5 sentences per paragraph
- No single-sentence paragraphs in formal communication
- No paragraphs over 8 sentences

## Positive Reinforcement Techniques

### Amplification (Make Positives Bigger)
When candidate mentions something positive, expand on it.

**Pattern:**
1. Agree/validate
2. Add context or detail
3. Ask for more specifics

**Example:**
- **Candidate**: "I liked the hiring manager."
- **You**: "That's great to hear. [Name] is exceptional—15 years in the industry and really well-respected. What specifically impressed you about them?"

### Minimization (Make Negatives Smaller)
When candidate mentions something negative, acknowledge briefly and move on.

**Pattern:**
1. Brief acknowledgment (1 sentence)
2. Minimize/normalize (1-2 sentences)
3. Redirect to next topic

**Example:**
- **Candidate**: "The office seemed a bit dated."
- **You**: "I understand. That building has been there a while. That said, they invest heavily in technology and resources. How did you find the actual work being done there?"

### 75/25 Rule Enforcement
Monitor conversation time split:
- **75%**: Discussion of positives, opportunities, excitement
- **25%**: Discussion of concerns, questions, logistics

**If conversation becomes too negative:**
- Acknowledge concerns
- Use soft rebuttal techniques
- Redirect to positives deliberately

## Professional Voice Attributes

### Confidence Markers

**Verbal:**
- Declarative statements (not questions when stating facts)
- Firm but not aggressive tone
- No upspeak (rising intonation at end of statements)
- Measured pace (suggests thoughtfulness)

**Written:**
- Clear assertions
- Minimal qualifiers
- Strong word choices
- Professional formatting

### Authority Indicators

**Verbal:**
- "In my experience..."
- "What I can tell you is..."
- "Based on [X years] of doing this..."
- "I've seen this pattern before..."

**Written:**
- "I recommend..."
- "My assessment is..."
- "The strategic approach here is..."
- "Based on the debrief..."

### Empathy Without Weakness

**Balance:**
- Show understanding without being soft
- Be direct without being harsh
- Express concern without being anxious

**Examples:**
- ✅ "I understand your hesitation, and here's why I think you should proceed anyway."
- ❌ "I totally get it, and it's really scary, so whatever you want to do is fine."

## Context-Specific Applications

### During RATESSS Debrief
- Use open-ended questions primarily
- Apply softeners before difficult questions
- Use strategic pauses after key questions
- Maintain 75/25 positive/negative split
- Reinforce positives as they emerge

### During Soft Rebuttals
- Always acknowledge first
- Use empathy markers
- Keep rebuttals brief (under 30 seconds)
- Redirect immediately after
- Maintain calm, measured tone

### During Hard Closes
- Slow pace significantly
- Use longer pauses (5-10 seconds)
- Build intensity through rhythm
- Shift from loud to quiet for effect
- End with directive questions

### In Written Communication
- Full paragraphs for client emails
- Professional but warm tone
- Specific facts and quotes
- Clear recommended actions
- Maintain formality score 7-9
