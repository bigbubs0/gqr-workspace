# Communication Rules & Standards
## Email Formatting and Tone Guidelines

This document defines the strict communication standards for all client-facing emails and internal notes generated through the Candidate Control Skill.

---

## CORE PRINCIPLES

### 1. Separation of Client-Facing and Internal Content

All emails must clearly distinguish between:
- **BLACK TEXT** = Client-ready content (can be copied and pasted without editing)
- **RED TEXT** = Internal notes for Account Manager only (never sent to client)

### 2. Professional Yet Conversational Tone

Strike a balance between:
- Professional credibility
- Warm, human connection
- Confidence without arrogance
- Enthusiasm without hyperbole

### 3. Positive Framing

Always lead with positives and frame concerns constructively:
- Acknowledge strengths first
- Address concerns delicately
- Reframe negatives when possible
- Never include purely negative feedback in client communication

---

## BLACK TEXT STANDARDS (Client-Facing)

### Purpose
Content that Account Managers can copy and paste directly to clients without any editing. Every word should be intentional and client-appropriate.

### Writing Requirements

#### Tone
- **Professional** - Proper grammar, complete sentences, no slang
- **Conversational** - Natural language, not robotic or overly formal
- **Confident** - Assert the candidate's value without hedging
- **Specific** - Use concrete examples and data points
- **Warm** - Genuine enthusiasm, not salesy

#### Structure
- **Full paragraphs** - Never use bullet points in client emails
- **Clear flow** - Logical progression of ideas
- **Appropriate length** - Thorough but concise (2-4 paragraphs typical)
- **Natural transitions** - Connect ideas smoothly

#### Content Rules

**Always Include:**
- Candidate's full name and years of experience
- Specific, quantifiable achievements
- Motivations for considering a move
- Why they're excited about THIS specific company/role
- Current role and responsibilities
- 2-3 compelling selling points tailored to the role

**Never Include:**
- Negative feedback or concerns
- Sensitive compensation details
- Visa status or authorization issues
- Information about competing processes
- Red flags or concerns
- Internal strategy discussions
- Anything that could hurt candidate's positioning

**Positive Framing Examples:**

❌ **Don't Say:**
"The candidate is concerned about the company's financial stability."

✅ **Do Say:**
"The candidate is eager to understand the company's growth trajectory and vision for the next 12 months."

---

❌ **Don't Say:**
"They're interviewing with three other companies and might have offers soon."

✅ **Do Say:**
"They're actively evaluating opportunities and appreciate the chance to learn more about your company."

---

❌ **Don't Say:**
"The candidate thinks the compensation might be too low."

✅ **Do Say:**
"The candidate is excited about the role and looking forward to understanding the total compensation package including equity."

### Example: CV Submission Email (BLACK TEXT)

```
Subject: Sarah Chen - 8 Years Enterprise SaaS Product Management

Hi [Client Name],

I'd like to introduce Sarah Chen, who brings 8 years of enterprise SaaS product management experience with a proven track record of scaling products from early stage to $50M+ ARR.

Sarah is currently a Senior Product Manager at TechCorp, where she leads a team of 5 managing a product line that has grown 20% year-over-year under her leadership. Before TechCorp, she was instrumental in bringing a B2B analytics platform from beta to Series B, working directly with the founding team to establish product-market fit. Her experience includes managing cross-functional teams, driving strategic roadmaps, and translating customer needs into product features - all of which align well with what you're looking for.

Sarah is exploring new opportunities because she's seeking a role where she can have greater strategic ownership and work at an earlier stage where her decisions have direct impact on company trajectory. She's particularly excited about your company's mission in the fintech space and the opportunity to shape product direction during this critical growth phase. The combination of your Series A stage, technical team, and market positioning is exactly what she's been seeking.

Please let me know if you'd like to move forward with a conversation.

Best regards,
[Recruiter Name]
```

### Example: Client Feedback Email (BLACK TEXT)

```
Subject: Interview Debrief - Sarah Chen

Hi [Client Name],

I just spoke with Sarah about yesterday's interview, and she was genuinely enthusiastic about the conversation. She was particularly impressed by the strategic vision you outlined for the product roadmap and felt there was strong alignment with her experience scaling enterprise products in similar market conditions.

Sarah specifically mentioned her conversation with Mark, your VP of Engineering, noting that she appreciated his collaborative approach and the technical depth of their discussion about architecture decisions. She also enjoyed speaking with Jennifer from the founding team and was energized by the company culture and mission-driven focus she observed throughout the day.

In terms of where this opportunity stands among her other options, she indicated that your company has moved to the top of her list. She's in late stages with two other companies and expects to receive an offer from one by November 15th, but she was clear that if you move forward, this would be her preferred choice. She's eager to hear your feedback and discuss next steps.

Please let me know your thoughts when you have a chance.

Best regards,
[Recruiter Name]
```

## RED TEXT STANDARDS (Internal Notes)

### Purpose
Sensitive information that the Account Manager needs to know but should never be shared with the client. This is where we flag risks, concerns, and strategic considerations.

### Content Requirements

**Always Include:**
- Visa/work authorization status and concerns
- Detailed compensation breakdown and expectations
- Competing interview processes with specific companies and timelines
- Red flags or concerns from intake/debrief
- Title expectations or sensitivities
- Any conditions for acceptance
- Closing strategy recommendations
- Risk factors

**Format:**
- Clear section headers
- Use 🔴 emoji or "RED TEXT" / "INTERNAL NOTES" header
- Bullet points acceptable for lists
- Be explicit - don't sugarcoat for internal notes
- Include specific numbers, dates, and company names

### Example: CV Submission Internal Notes (RED TEXT)

```
🔴 INTERNAL NOTES FOR AM - DO NOT SEND TO CLIENT

VISA STATUS:
- US Citizen - no concerns

COMPENSATION:
- Current: $180K base + $40K bonus (80% attainment) + $80K equity/year = $300K total
- Target: $220K base minimum, $350K+ total comp preferred
- Strong equity package could offset lower cash
- Current equity vests through March 2026 - may want sign-on to offset

COMPETING PROCESSES:
- CompanyA (competitor): Final round scheduled 11/12, expects offer by 11/15
- CompanyB (startup): Second round, offer timeline approximately 11/20-11/25
- CompanyA is currently her #1 choice (we are #2)

RED FLAGS / CONCERNS:
- Needs to see clear path to VP level within 18 months
- Concerned about Series A stage - prefers Series B+ for stability
- May require higher comp than client's budgeted range ($200K base max)
- Strong opinions about product strategy - could conflict with founder's vision

TITLE EXPECTATIONS:
- Wants "Senior Product Manager" minimum
- Will not accept "Product Manager" title (step backward)

START DATE:
- 3-week notice period
- Would prefer 4 weeks to transition properly
- Earliest possible: December 1st
- Has $15K bonus that pays December 15th - may want to wait

CLOSING STRATEGY:
- Emphasize equity upside and growth trajectory
- Highlight path to VP (ask client about timeline)
- Move quickly - CompanyA offer coming 11/15
- Consider offering flexibility on start date to preserve bonus
```

## TONE GUIDELINES

### Appropriate Tone Markers

**Professional:**
- Complete sentences
- Proper punctuation
- No typos or errors
- Correct titles and names
- Formal greetings and closings

**Conversational:**
- Natural language flow
- Contractions are okay ("they're" not "they are")
- Warm, human voice
- Not robotic or template-like

**Confident:**
- Assertive statements about candidate quality
- "Sarah brings exceptional experience" not "Sarah has some good experience"
- Definitive language when appropriate
- Not hedging unnecessarily ("I think maybe possibly...")

**Specific:**
- Use numbers and data
- "20% year-over-year growth" not "strong growth"
- Name specific technologies, companies, methodologies
- Concrete examples over generalities

### Pace and Flow

**Slow Pace:**
- Don't rush through information
- Give each idea room to breathe
- One main idea per paragraph
- Smooth transitions between thoughts

**Not Rushed:**
- Avoid breathless run-on sentences
- Don't cram too much into one paragraph
- Create white space visually
- Let important points stand alone

### Word Choice

**Positive Language:**
- "Eager to" not "willing to"
- "Seeking" not "leaving because of"
- "Excited about" not "interested in"
- "Particularly impressed" not "liked"

**Avoid Weak Qualifiers:**
- ❌ "somewhat," "fairly," "pretty," "kind of"
- ✅ Use concrete descriptors or omit

**Avoid Hedging:**
- ❌ "I think," "perhaps," "possibly," "might"
- ✅ State directly unless genuinely uncertain

**Avoid Jargon:**
- ❌ "Synergistic," "paradigm shift," "thought leader"
- ✅ Clear, specific language

## FORMATTING STANDARDS

### Email Structure

**Subject Line:**
- Clear and descriptive
- Include candidate name
- Keep under 50 characters

**Opening:**
- Professional greeting
- Context-setting first sentence
- Get to the point quickly

**Body:**
- 2-4 paragraphs typical
- Each paragraph = one main idea
- Logical flow
- White space between paragraphs

**Closing:**
- Clear call to action
- Professional sign-off
- Full contact information

### Paragraph Construction

**First Paragraph:**
- Who the candidate is
- Years of experience
- Primary expertise area

**Middle Paragraphs:**
- Current role and achievements
- Relevant background
- Why they're exploring a move
- Why they're excited about this opportunity

**Final Paragraph:**
- Ranking or competitive positioning
- Next steps or call to action
- Timeline if relevant

### Length Guidelines

**CV Submission Email:**
- 3-4 paragraphs
- 300-400 words
- 2-3 minutes to read

**Client Feedback Email:**
- 3-4 paragraphs
- 250-350 words
- 2 minutes to read

**Internal Notes:**
- As long as needed
- Bullet points acceptable
- Include all relevant details

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### In Client-Facing Communication

❌ **Bullet points instead of paragraphs**
- Feels like a resume, not a thoughtful introduction
- Loses narrative flow and connection

❌ **Including red flags or concerns**
- "The candidate is worried about your runway"
- "They need visa sponsorship"
- Save for internal notes only

❌ **Vague descriptions**
- "Good experience in the field"
- "Nice person who would fit well"
- Use specific, concrete details

❌ **Overselling**
- "The perfect unicorn candidate!"
- "You won't find anyone better!"
- Be confident but realistic

❌ **Generic templated language**
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "Please find attached"
- Sound human and natural

### In Internal Notes

❌ **Sugarcoating concerns**
- Be direct about red flags
- AM needs to know risks clearly

❌ **Missing critical details**
- Always include specific numbers
- Name competing companies
- Document exact timelines

❌ **Forgetting to separate from client content**
- Never let internal notes get sent to client
- Always use clear markers and separation

## VOICE AND STYLE EXAMPLES

### Strong Example

"Sarah brings 8 years of enterprise product management experience with consistent results scaling products from early stage through Series B. At TechCorp, she's led her product line to 20% year-over-year growth while managing a team of 5 and working cross-functionally with engineering, sales, and customer success. She's particularly drawn to your company's approach to fintech innovation and sees this as an opportunity to apply her experience in a high-growth environment where she can have strategic impact."

**Why it works:**
- Specific numbers and details
- Concrete achievements
- Natural, confident flow
- Clear value proposition
- Tailored to the role

### Weak Example

"Sarah is a pretty good product manager with some solid experience. She's been at TechCorp for a while and has done well there. She seems interested in your company and thinks it might be a good fit. Let me know if you'd like to talk to her."

**Why it doesn't work:**
- Vague and generic
- Weak qualifiers ("pretty good," "some")
- No specific details
- Lacks confidence
- Not compelling

## HANDLING DIFFICULT SITUATIONS

### When Candidate Has Concerns

**Don't:**
- Include concerns in client email
- Ignore concerns completely
- Minimize legitimate issues

**Do:**
- Flag concerns in internal notes
- Suggest ways to address
- Provide context for AM
- Recommend proactive solutions

### When Information is Missing

**Don't:**
- Use placeholder text ([TBD])
- Make up information
- Leave obvious gaps

**Do:**
- Note what's missing in internal section
- Explain why it's missing
- Plan to gather information
- Set timeline for follow-up

### When Feedback is Negative

**Don't:**
- Include in client communication
- Ignore completely
- Sugarcoat in internal notes

**Do:**
- Document fully in internal notes
- Assess if it's a dealbreaker
- Consider if it can be addressed
- Include in closing strategy

## CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT

### Self-Assessment Questions

After drafting any communication, ask:
1. Could the AM copy and paste this directly to the client without editing?
2. Is every claim specific and supported?
3. Would the client feel confident about this candidate?
4. Are all concerns appropriately flagged internally?
5. Is the tone professional yet warm?
6. Does it flow naturally?
7. Is it the right length?
8. Would I send this if it were my company?

### Getting Better

- Review past successful submissions
- Learn from AM feedback
- Study effective communication examples
- Practice writing more concisely
- Refine your positive framing skills
- Build vocabulary of strong descriptors
