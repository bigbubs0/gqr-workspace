# Client Feedback Email Template

## Purpose
This template guides the creation of professional post-interview feedback emails to clients. These emails must reassure the client of candidate interest while accurately representing the candidate's impressions and concerns.

---

## Email Structure

### Subject Line Format
```
[Candidate Full Name] - Post-Interview Feedback: [Position Title]
```

**Examples:**
- "Michael Chen - Post-Interview Feedback: Senior Financial Analyst"
- "Sarah Rodriguez - Post-Interview Feedback: VP of Operations"

---

## Full Email Template

```
Subject: [Candidate Name] - Post-Interview Feedback: [Position Title]

Dear [Client First Name],

I spoke with [Candidate Name] following [his/her/their] interview yesterday with [your organization/you and the team]. I wanted to provide you with comprehensive feedback from our debrief conversation.

[INTEREST LEVEL PARAGRAPH]
[Candidate Name] expressed [strong interest/genuine interest/positive interest] in the opportunity and was [impressed by/enthusiastic about/encouraged by] [specific aspects]. [He/She/They] particularly appreciated [mention 2-3 specific positives from the interview]. 

[IMPRESSIONS OF INDIVIDUALS - CRITICAL]
In terms of the team, [Candidate Name] was [positively impressed by/found excellent chemistry with/appreciated the expertise of] [specific individuals met]. [He/She/They] noted that [Hiring Manager Name] was [specific positive quality - e.g., "exceptionally knowledgeable about market dynamics" or "clearly articulate about the vision for the role"]. [Additional specific impressions of other team members if mentioned].

[ROLE ALIGNMENT]
[Candidate Name] sees strong alignment between [his/her/their] background in [specific experience] and the [specific aspects of the role]. [He/She/They] was particularly excited about [specific responsibility or opportunity] and feels [his/her/their] experience with [relevant background] positions [him/her/them] well to [deliver specific value].

[QUESTIONS/AREAS FOR EXPLORATION - If Applicable]
[Candidate Name] did mention wanting to explore [specific topic] in more depth in a potential second meeting. [He/She/They] is particularly interested in understanding [specific question or concern area]. This is a natural next step in [his/her/their] evaluation process.

[COMPETITIVE POSITIONING - CRITICAL]
In terms of timing, I should note that [Candidate Name] is [actively interviewing with/in final rounds with/considering an offer from] [number] other firm(s). [Include specific detail if relevant: "Company X is a front-runner, though [Candidate Name] expressed that your opportunity offers stronger [specific advantage]." OR "While other processes are underway, [Candidate Name] indicated that your organization is [his/her/their] top choice given [specific reasons]."]

[NEXT STEPS PARAGRAPH]
[Candidate Name] is very interested in moving forward and is available for a second interview [provide specific availability: "next week, with Tuesday through Thursday working best" or "any day next week with advance notice"]. [If relevant: "[He/She/They] would appreciate the opportunity to meet with [specific person] and/or [see/discuss] [specific aspect]"].

[CLOSING - RECOMMENDATION]
Based on my conversation with [Candidate Name], I believe [he/she/they] would be an excellent fit for this role and brings the [specific qualities] you're looking for. I recommend moving forward with a second interview [if you agree]. Please let me know your thoughts on next steps and timing.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Your Title]
[Contact Information]
```

## Paragraph-by-Paragraph Guidance

### 1. Interest Level Paragraph
**Purpose**: Immediately reassure client that candidate is interested

**Key Elements:**
- Clear statement of interest level
- Specific positives mentioned by candidate
- Enthusiastic but professional tone

**Calibration:**
- **High interest**: "expressed strong interest," "was enthusiastic about," "clearly excited by"
- **Moderate interest**: "expressed genuine interest," "was impressed by," "saw strong potential in"
- **Lower interest** (but proceeding): "expressed interest in learning more," "was intrigued by," "appreciated the opportunity to"

**What to Include:**
- 2-3 specific things candidate mentioned positively
- Overall impression of the company/role
- Any spontaneous enthusiasm indicators

**Example:**
```
Michael expressed strong interest in the Senior Analyst position and was particularly enthusiastic about the firm's quantitative approach to portfolio management. He was impressed by the sophistication of your modeling infrastructure and the intellectual caliber of the team he met.
```

### 2. Impressions of Individuals (CRITICAL)
**Purpose**: Show candidate engaged with and respected the people they met

**Key Elements:**
- Specific names of people met
- Specific positive qualities mentioned
- Direct quotes if particularly strong

**Why This Matters:**
- Validates client's team
- Shows candidate was paying attention
- Builds rapport between client and candidate

**What to Include:**
- Hiring manager's specific positive qualities (mandatory)
- Other team members if mentioned
- Actual descriptors used by candidate when possible

**Examples:**
```
✅ GOOD:
"Sarah was very impressed by James Murphy's strategic thinking and depth of knowledge about emerging markets. She noted that his 15-year background in the sector came through clearly in their discussion. She also appreciated the collaborative dynamic she observed between James and the senior analyst team."

❌ WEAK:
"Sarah liked everyone she met and thought they were nice."
```

### 3. Role Alignment Paragraph
**Purpose**: Show candidate sees fit between their skills and role requirements

**Key Elements:**
- Specific experience they mentioned as relevant
- Specific aspects of role they're excited about
- Value they believe they can deliver

**Strategic Function:**
- Reinforces that candidate understands the role
- Shows candidate has valuable relevant experience
- Plants seeds of what they'd contribute

**Example:**
```
Michael sees strong alignment between his five years of quantitative analysis at Fidelity and your need for sophisticated risk modeling. He was particularly excited about building out the factor attribution framework you discussed and feels his recent work implementing similar systems positions him well to accelerate that initiative.
```

### 4. Questions/Areas for Exploration (If Applicable)
**Purpose**: Surface concerns or information needs without alarming client

**Key Elements:**
- Frame as natural due diligence
- Specific topics they want to explore
- Positive framing ("wants to learn more" not "is concerned about")

**When to Include:**
- Candidate has legitimate questions that need answering
- Second interview makes sense to address these
- Questions are reasonable and addressable

**When to Omit:**
- Questions are minor or already answered
- Questions might alarm client unnecessarily
- Can be handled separately

**Examples:**
```
✅ GOOD:
"Sarah mentioned wanting to explore the team's approach to work-life balance in more depth in a potential second meeting. She's particularly interested in understanding how the firm manages peak periods and travel expectations."

✅ GOOD:
"Michael would like to better understand the firm's three-year growth trajectory and how the analyst role might evolve as the team scales."

❌ TOO NEGATIVE:
"Sarah expressed concerns about whether the culture would be a good fit and questioned if the compensation would be sufficient."
```

### 5. Competitive Positioning (CRITICAL)
**Purpose**: Create urgency and inform client of competitive dynamics

**Key Elements:**
- Specific information about other processes
- Where client ranks in candidate's thinking
- Timeline pressure if relevant

**Critical Balance:**
- Be truthful but strategic
- Create urgency without appearing pushy
- Position client favorably when possible

**Scenarios and Examples:**

**Scenario A: Your client is the front-runner**
```
"In terms of timing, I should note that Michael is in final rounds with two other firms - a boutique investment manager and a larger asset management company. However, he indicated that your opportunity offers the strongest combination of intellectual challenge and growth potential, making it his top choice at this stage."
```

**Scenario B: Client is competitive but not leading**
```
"I should mention that Michael is also interviewing with Morgan Stanley and is in final rounds there. While that process is further along, Michael expressed that your firm's entrepreneurial environment and quantitative focus represent a unique opportunity that he's very interested in pursuing."
```

**Scenario C: Client is behind but viable**
```
"Michael is actively interviewing with several firms and has one offer in hand from a competitor, though the decision timeline extends through next week. He expressed strong interest in your opportunity and would welcome the chance to progress to a second interview before making his final decision."
```

**Scenario D: No competition**
```
"Michael is currently focused on this opportunity and is eager to learn more through a second interview. He's not actively pursuing other searches at this time."
```

### 6. Next Steps Paragraph
**Purpose**: Facilitate scheduling and maintain momentum

**Key Elements:**
- Explicit statement of interest in proceeding
- Specific availability
- Any specific requests for second interview

**What to Include:**
- Exact availability (days/times if provided)
- Willingness to be flexible if needed
- Any specific people they want to meet
- Any specific topics they want to cover

**Example:**
```
Michael is very interested in moving forward and is available for a second interview next week, with Tuesday through Thursday working best for his schedule. He would appreciate the opportunity to meet with Linda Chen (CTO) and to see the quantitative research infrastructure you mentioned. He's flexible on format (in-person or video) based on your preference.
```

### 7. Closing Recommendation
**Purpose**: Provide your professional assessment and suggested action

**Key Elements:**
- Clear recommendation (if appropriate)
- Specific reasons supporting recommendation
- Call to action for client

**When to Recommend Proceeding:**
- Candidate is genuinely interested
- Fit appears strong based on debrief
- No major red flags emerged

**When to Be Neutral:**
- Unclear fit
- Candidate has significant concerns to address
- Need to see client's assessment first

**Examples:**
```
✅ STRONG RECOMMENDATION:
"Based on my conversation with Michael, I believe he would be an excellent fit for this role and brings the quantitative rigor and strategic thinking you're looking for. I strongly recommend moving forward with a second interview. Please let me know your thoughts on next steps and timing."

✅ MODERATE RECOMMENDATION:
"Michael appears to be a strong potential fit for this role, particularly given his quantitative background and interest in your market approach. I recommend a second interview to explore the strategic planning aspects of the role in more depth. Please let me know how you'd like to proceed."

✅ NEUTRAL (when concerns exist):
"I'd welcome the opportunity to discuss Michael's feedback in more detail and hear your impressions from the interview. Please let me know when you're available for a brief call to align on next steps."
```

## Quality Control Checklist

Before sending client feedback email, verify:

**Content Requirements:**
- [ ] Subject line includes candidate name and position
- [ ] Interest level clearly stated in first paragraph
- [ ] At least 2-3 specific positives mentioned
- [ ] Impressions of individuals included (especially hiring manager)
- [ ] Specific role alignment described
- [ ] Competitive situation addressed (if relevant)
- [ ] Next steps and availability provided
- [ ] Your recommendation or suggested action included

**Tone Requirements:**
- [ ] Professional but warm (Formality Score 7-9)
- [ ] Full paragraphs, not bullet points
- [ ] Reassuring about candidate interest
- [ ] Transparent about concerns without being alarming
- [ ] Action-oriented and forward-looking

**Strategic Requirements:**
- [ ] Positions candidate favorably
- [ ] Creates appropriate urgency if needed
- [ ] Surfaces important questions without creating doubt
- [ ] Maintains your credibility (honest assessment)
- [ ] Facilitates next steps

**Technical Requirements:**
- [ ] All names spelled correctly
- [ ] Pronouns used correctly
- [ ] No grammatical errors
- [ ] No jargon or filler words
- [ ] Professional email signature included

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Too Generic
❌ **Bad**: "The candidate liked everyone and is interested in the role."

✅ **Good**: "Sarah was particularly impressed by James Murphy's strategic vision for international expansion and appreciated the collaborative dynamic she observed in the team meeting."

### 2. Too Negative
❌ **Bad**: "The candidate has significant concerns about compensation and isn't sure about the fit."

✅ **Good**: "Sarah would like to understand the full compensation structure, including long-term incentive components, in more detail during a second discussion."

### 3. Bullet Points Instead of Prose
❌ **Bad**: 
```
Candidate feedback:
• Liked the hiring manager
• Interested in the role
• Has other interviews
• Available next week
```

✅ **Good**: [Use full paragraph format as shown in template]

### 4. Missing Competitive Context
❌ **Bad**: Failing to mention that candidate has other offers or is in final rounds elsewhere

✅ **Good**: Always include competitive positioning paragraph when relevant

### 5. Vague Availability
❌ **Bad**: "The candidate is available for a second interview sometime soon."

✅ **Good**: "Michael is available Tuesday through Thursday of next week, with mornings working best."

### 6. No Recommendation
❌ **Bad**: Ending with "Let me know what you think."

✅ **Good**: "I recommend moving forward with a second interview. Please let me know your thoughts on timing."

## Length Guidelines

**Optimal Length:**
- 4-7 paragraphs
- 300-500 words total
- Each paragraph 3-5 sentences

**Too Short (<250 words):**
- Appears rushed or uncommitted
- Lacks specificity
- Misses strategic elements

**Too Long (>600 words):**
- Client won't read carefully
- Key points get buried
- Seems unfocused

---

## Adaptation for Different Scenarios

### Scenario: Candidate Very Interested, No Concerns
**Focus:**
- Lead with strong enthusiasm
- Detailed impressions of individuals
- Competitive pressure (if applicable)
- Strong recommendation

### Scenario: Candidate Interested But Has Questions
**Focus:**
- Balance interest with questions
- Frame questions as reasonable due diligence
- Suggest second interview to address
- Moderate recommendation

### Scenario: Candidate Lukewarm
**Focus:**
- Present interest honestly but positively
- Identify what could increase interest
- Suggest what client could address
- Neutral position pending client's interest

### Scenario: Multiple Strong Competing Offers
**Focus:**
- Create urgency immediately
- Emphasize what differentiates client
- Aggressive timeline for next steps
- Strong recommendation with urgency
